🌿 Bioregenerative life support

Keep a crew alive with a living habitat

On Mars there's no resupply run. A Bioregenerative Life-Support System (BLSS) closes the loop — plants and machines recycle air, water, and food so the colony sustains itself. Learn how it works, then design one and run the model.

The concept

What is a bioregenerative life-support system?

A habitat on the Moon or Mars has to provide every life-support function — breathable air, clean water, and food — with almost no resupply from Earth. The answer is a regenerative, closed-loop system: crew breathe out CO₂ and produce waste; crops take up that CO₂, transpire water, and grow food while releasing O₂; chemical systems scrub, split, and recycle what's left. The tighter the loop, the less mass you must launch — and launch mass is the dominant cost of any space mission.

This page is built on the BioSim model (Kortenkamp & Bell, TRACLabs / NASA), a simulation of an integrated habitat and its life-support modules that AI-research groups use to study how to design and control these systems. Your BioSim Astrobotany branch extends it for plant-focused (astrobotany) scenarios.

The link to the game. In Settlers of Mars, "Living on Mars" means building a water mine and starting a farm. BLSS is the science under that turn — and the Farmer & Scientist role uses exactly these models to size the habitat and farm.
Inside the model

The modules that keep the loop closed

BioSim models the habitat as interacting process modules — each consumes some resources and produces others.

🧑‍🚀

Crew

Consume O₂, water, and food; produce CO₂, waste water, and solid waste. Rates depend on crew size and activity (sleep, work, EVA).

🌬️

Air revitalization

Scrubs CO₂ from the cabin (VCCR), reacts it with H₂ to reclaim water (CRS), then splits water back into O₂ (OGS).

💧

Water recovery

Turns dirty and grey water back into potable water, recovering condensate from crew and crop transpiration.

🌱

Biomass / crops

Nine+ crops (wheat, potato, soybean, lettuce, tomato…) take up CO₂ and water and light, releasing O₂ and growing edible food.

⚡

Power

Solar (variable) or nuclear (steady) supplies every module. Lighting a big crop area is a major power — and mass — cost.

🛢️

Stores

Buffers of O₂, water, and food absorb the day-to-day imbalance. Bigger buffers are safer but add launch mass.

Habitat design

Where this is going

From sandbox to research tool

🤖 Machine-learning habitat optimisation

Every saved run is a labelled data point: a habitat configuration and its outcome (sustainable? mission length? system mass?). That's exactly the setup the BioSim paper uses genetic algorithms and reinforcement learning for — searching a huge design space to minimise Equivalent System Mass (ESM) while keeping the crew alive. The roadmap: let an optimiser propose habitat designs — scale, crop area, and the crop/life-content ratio — and learn which trade-offs win.

🎓 In the classroom (AIRI course)

BioSim Lab is designed to slot into the AIRI course as a hands-on module: students design a habitat, defend their trade-offs, export their runs, and then pit a machine-learning optimiser against their best human design.

As a new station on the Settlers of Mars board, BioSim Lab turns the "Living on Mars" turn into a real engineering decision — the Farmer & Scientist sizes the farm here, and the whole crew lives or dies by the result.
